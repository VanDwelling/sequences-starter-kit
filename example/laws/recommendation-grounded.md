## What it checks

The core recommendation follows from the analysis presented in the deliverable, not from unstated assumptions or generic best practice.

## Why it matters

A recommendation that isn't grounded in the analysis it sits next to is actually two separate documents pretending to be one. The analysis gives the client confidence that you understand their situation. The recommendation tells them what to do. If the recommendation doesn't *follow from* the analysis, the client has no reason to trust it — it could have been written before the analysis was done (and often was).

## What passing looks like

The evaluator can trace a clear reasoning path from the data/observations in the analysis to the recommendation. The recommendation cites or refers to specific findings. A reader who agreed with the analysis would find it difficult to arrive at a substantially different recommendation.

## What failing looks like

- The recommendation could have been written without the analysis (it's generic enough to apply to any client)
- The analysis surfaces findings that the recommendation doesn't address
- The recommendation introduces considerations not present in the analysis
- The logical step from "here's what we found" to "here's what you should do" requires assumptions that aren't stated

## Evaluator guidance

This is not about whether the recommendation is *correct* — the evaluator isn't a domain expert. It's about whether the recommendation is *supported by the document's own analysis*. The test is internal consistency, not external truth.

A recommendation may legitimately introduce domain knowledge not present in the analysis (e.g., "based on our experience with similar migrations..."). That's acceptable as long as the deliverable is transparent about it. What fails is when the recommendation *appears* to follow from the analysis but actually doesn't.
