## What it checks

The deliverable ends with concrete, time-bound next steps that the client can act on.

## Why it matters

A deliverable without next steps is a status update, not a recommendation. The client finishes reading and asks "so what do I do now?" — which means the deliverable failed at its core job. Next steps are where the recommendation becomes operational. Vague next steps ("consider implementing...") are marginally better than no next steps, but they still push the synthesis work onto the client.

## What passing looks like

The deliverable includes a next steps section (or equivalent closing) where each step has:
- A specific action (not "consider" or "explore" but "schedule", "approve", "assign", "migrate")
- An owner or responsible party (even if it's "your team" — the point is that someone is named)
- A timeframe (this week, by Q3, within 30 days — specific enough to be trackable)

## What failing looks like

- No next steps section at all
- Next steps that are restatements of the recommendation ("implement the recommended approach")
- Next steps without owners ("the migration should be planned")
- Next steps without timeframes ("at some point, the team should...")
- Next steps that require another deliverable before they're actionable ("a detailed plan will follow")

## Evaluator guidance

The bar is not project-management-grade detail. It's "could the client forward this section to their team and have someone start working on Monday?" If the answer is no, the next steps are too vague.

For proposals specifically: the first next step should always be about the decision to proceed, not about the work itself. "Approve this proposal by [date]" or "Schedule a kick-off for the week of [date]." The client needs to know what *their* first action is.
